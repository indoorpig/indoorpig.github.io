Visionary

UX Design
August 2019
3 Weeks

Finding Safety




In 2016, Mercedes-Benz began its dive into future connected car services. In the years since, they still lack products that focus on user needs.

I designed a solution for connected car monitoring with the Innovation Team at MBUSA.

Background



Mercedes-Benz’s vision of the future revolves around CASE — Connected, Autonomous, Shared Services, Electric.

As a result, a team pitched the idea of using inactive cameras of select models.* This idea gained traction but lacked a team to investigate further.

Most importantly: there was no clear need that a camera system would address. What problem would this service solve?

*Information obfuscated for confidential reasons.

What problem would this service solve?

Challenge




Provide control and peace of mind.


Our Innovation Team stepped in to deliver a product concept using onboard cameras and create meaningful impact for drivers.

We had a timeline of 3 weeks to create technical and design prototypes. Our goal was to get our concept approved and funded by a GM to continue development and testing.

My Role



I led UX design in our team of four interns (1 design, 1 product, 2 developers) and facilitated the process of synthesizing research into product strategy.

I conducted user research along with my team, injecting a design perspective into an organization which lacked this point-of-view.

We were guided by the three full-time Innovation Team members — David Owen specifically helped formulate design choices.

This project successfully gained approval of two GMs in August 2019. The project is ongoing based on our work.

The Problem




Finding Authentic Demand



The Innovation Hub team was created on the basis of finding authentic user needs and addressing them. Our process reflected this principle.

While it seemed natural to start with our eventual concept of a monitoring product, our concept was forged through user research.

In particular, the examination of driver relationships with their cars revealed insights into the desires of users.

Cracks Appear



Diving into the driver-car dynamic highlighted differences between what drivers say and what they do.

When asked explicitly, drivers reported no anxiety about the risk of their cars getting damaged. Drivers seemed apathetic or downright dismissive about monitoring their cars.

Yet small behavioral inconsistencies popped up through certain user interviews:

Safety Over Convenience


Some drivers opted to park further away from other cars in parking lots.

Confirmed Safety Over First Spot Available


Some drivers looked up parking in new areas.

Covered Over Street


Most drivers opted to park in garages rather than on the street.

Discoveries



Drivers were taking small actions to limit incidents — with limited effectiveness. Of course they understood there were factors beyond their control.

But major painpoints were occurring when drivers had no sense of agency at all — a complete lack of control in situations where they expected some sort of recourse.

major painpoints were occurring when drivers had no sense of agency at all

Context of driving and parking mattered too, affecting driver decision-making and how painpoints manifested. Four major areas were identified:

  1. Driving or Parking in the City
  2. Driving or Parking in Unfamiliar Territory
  3. Driving with Shared Vehicle
  4. Social Explorations

Context Matters


Driver painpoints evolve depending on the context of their driving. That context includes driving activity, location, and who is around.

Defined Problem



“Fine until it’s not” came to define the problem.

Drivers don’t think they need security because of the high barrier for entry with aftersales solutions. They preferred taking the risk of their car getting damaged.

At the same time, drivers want more control over the safety of their vehicles. Mixed-success coping methods to avoid damage were evidence of this.

But ultimately if incidents occurred, drivers had no options, amplifying already negative experiences into a mini-crises.

How can we provide drivers control and assurance over the safety of their Mercedes-Benz vehicle?

The Solution




Visionary



Visionary is a monitoring and video capture platform for select Mercedes-Benz models.

A Vigilant Safety Net



Visionary monitors your car, logging events, pushing notifications, and recording incidents for drivers.

The Right Security for the Right Time



Drivers set how sensitive they want Visionary to be based on thresholds for G-sensor, motion, and video input. Parking in your garage at home? Turn Visionary off. Handing the car off to a valet? Adjust Visionary to be more alert.

Keep Track of What’s Going On



Visionary logs all events in one place, giving users an accurate timeline of events that have happened to their car with relevant information and videos.

On-Demand View



With Live View users can check in on their car at any time. If drivers receive an incident notification, they can immediately see what’s going on in the live stream.

Power to the Driver



Drivers have even more control in starting manual video captures or capturing the last two minutes.

How Did I Get Here




Document First



Our Innovation team was particularly interested in reducing confirmation bias. Documenting assumptions helped prevent hindsight bias and framing the content of our user interviews.

Before organizing user research, we documented expected areas of opportunity for onboard cameras, situations in which drivers may utilize them, and the types of drivers that might use them.

Can We Just Talk



Our team talked to 20 drivers across 2 days. Our team used strategies (developed by the Center for Deliberate Innovation, formerly Flashpoint, at Georgia Tech) to avoid bias.

The goal was to investigate driver-car relationships. Cues like car nicknames (e.g. Jerald the Jeep), preferred parking spots, and other personal rituals grounded real behavior patterns, as opposed to self-perceptions.

Precise wording mattered. We designed each question to reveal an area of interest. In turn, each of these led to a greater insight into user needs.

Asking the Right Questions


Some of the questions we asked. There was intent behind each question and could be traced to a perception about users.

Making a Snapshot



At the end of the first week, I designed and led a team workshop. The goal was to transform qualitative data into an initial snapshot of users. We could then stress-test this with more research.

I emphasized the dangers of creating overly detailed personas too early. And to be more inclusive in our product, I pushed our team to view personas as spectrums rather than as unique identifiers.

I ran affinity mapping to sort persona types. 3 dynamics were identified:

  1. Comfortable Drivers vs. Anxious Drivers
  2. Driving for Leisure vs. Daily Drivers
  3. Alert Drivers vs. Distracted Drivers
We documented each persona type’s needs and our assumptions about them. To wrap up, we ran a quick ideation activity.

Iterative Research



Over the next 2 days, we talked to another 14 drivers. We investigated confusing nuances and unknowns from the previous workshop.

Afterwards we met again for another workshop. We folded in the new research, survey, and qualitative data for a better snapshot. This led to a number of tweaks and added a new dynamic:

  1. Comfortable Drivers vs. Anxious Drivers
  2. Driving for Leisure vs. Daily Drivers
  3. Alert Drivers vs. Distracted Drivers
  4. Value Car Highly vs. Ambivalent About Car

We re-shuffled persona types with this new information. We matched possible situations (car got dinged) and contexts (Trader Joe’s parking lot) with each persona group while using situation - motivation - outcome mapping.

These exercises helped highlight 2 groups with compelling user stories that we determined to be our target customers:



Reverent-Exploratory Drivers

These drivers value their car high and remain alert while driving (“reverent”). They feel comfortable in their frequent drives for leisure (“exploratory”). Their need is having more control when it comes to monitoring and capturing video.

Careful-Unconcerned Drivers

These drivers feel comfortable driving and realize the value of their car (“careful”). But they weren’t necessarily the most alert drivers and only drove for daily commutes (“unconcerned”). For them, getting status updates and assurance in case of damage is most important.

At Last...



Based on user needs, we determined a monitoring system as a solution had to have certain qualities:

  • Integrated: It must be a solution that works with MB vehicles without extensive setup
  • Connected: It must be an online system, preferably with a cellular data package
  • Event-logging: It must be able to record incidents, note relevant information, and log all information in an accessible format
We split into our individual roles after we defined features and scope as a team.

Design




Establishing Behavioral Motivations



To start, I had to set driver motivations on a behavioral level. The basis for this came from existing behaviors drivers were already exhibiting, as highlighted in journey maps:

  1. Analyze: Observe context and risk of incidents
  2. Act: Perceptibly take action to prevent an incident
  3. Feedback: Receive continued assurance and status
  4. Review: Secure recourse in the case of an incident

Early Explorations



I had to move fast in this last week. Early designs built off this behavior. Information architecture was enacted through card sorting to create initial frameworks.

Revising Information Architecture


Better understanding of user mental models came through card sorting in 2 iterations. More time would allow for a formal card sorting study.

Security and video reviewing functions in discrete tabs. The central dashboard became the main focus for capturing, checking updates, and adjusting the sensitivity of monitoring.

Paper Prototypes


Inherent lo-fi framing provided actionable feedback. 3 iterations evolved based on feedback; specifically, eliminating redundancies and de-emphasizing features.

Oversight Leads to Insight



Quick and dirty rounds of usability testing resulted in a number of stepwise improvements.

Incremental Revisions


Cursory usability testing revealed small improvements for the then-called Security mode.

But there was a larger problem I downplayed: contextual sensitivity. Driver mental models and concerns were framed around their situational relationship to their car at a given moment.

Those situations could be simple: driving versus parking. Or more granular: parking in a garage versus the street. Even more granular: how often drivers park on a given street.

I missed the mark in the nuance of driver expectations in specific situations; the design needed to accommodate for the complexity. In the next few iterations:

  • I split the dashboard into distinct “Park” and “Drive” modes.
  • I fleshed out the sensitivity of parking modes.
  • I re-framed the event log and video library together as post-hoc, reviewing actions.

Expanding Context Sensitivity


The challenge was keeping the flow streamlined while expanding capacity to deal with situational needs. (Left) Distinct Drive/Park modes inspired action-based, context-sensitive use. Note the updated monitoring sensitivity slider. (Right) Event Log/Library as post-hoc actions were consolidated.

Visualize Visionary



Heading into the final days of the sprint, I implemented a cohesive visual design, moving away from the standardized MB app designs for internal reasons.

Visuals were focused on the Visionary theme of shedding light onto the dark, creating high contrast between dark tones and sparse highlights. As a result, the metallic, silver theme of MB was allowed to “shine.”

Small visual details like the car silhouette subtly indicated user-specific details like the model of the paired car. A new icon set helped define features. Sparse color was used — blue for highlighted status information or yellow destructive/warning indications.

Defining Visual Language


Above are two early explorations in utilizing a visual system to enhance use.

Design for Assurance



The main focus was still providing assurance to drivers in each facet of use.

The Drive interface emphasizes manual capturing and on/off for monitoring, cutting down on excess information while in the act of driving.

Alternatively the Park interface’s hierarchy emphasizes monitoring sensitivity and status updates. Drivers can also view visual crowdsourced incident data to assess the safety of a nearby area and adjust sensitivity accordingly.

The event log displays relevant incident information. Functionally users here can flag key incidents, playback and download video, and extract video data — validated by developer teammates’ technical demos.

From these key elements, I formed the flow of Visionary.

Next Steps




Ultimately my design and the project (owned by the Innovation team and another internal team) gained approval from managers. Continued testing, refinement, and development is still needed. My recommendations for next steps:

Usability Testing



At the end of our three weeks, I didn’t have nearly as much time as I wanted to test my prototypes. Clarifying their mental models, especially in more immersive, context-sensitive environments would deliver better ways to design for drivers. In particular, I would investigate user mental models for notification pushes, status updates, and sensitivity adjustment.

Copywriting



Content written on my part was not as well thought-out and lacked user feedback. My managers’ dedicated help with copywriting was an improvement, but I believe even more work could be done to clarify different monitoring states and actions.

User Research



As we get more user feedback, it’s essential to re-evaluate the needs of drivers. The user research we laid out was a cursory 2-week process that only laid a foundation. That work needs validation and heavy refinement from both qualitative and quantitative sources.

Lessons Learned




Design Impact



At MBUSA, I didn’t have a design team. I was lucky to have an incredibly receptive team and manager who had invaluable feedback.

Design's value lies in activating a user-centric approach within a multidisciplinary team. Empowering non-designers meant just as much as delivering and evaluating prototypes.

Addressing user needs is creating business value.

Leverage People-Resources



Not talking to other related internal stakeholders until midway into the sprint resulted in a pivot. Identify experts and leverage their knowledge to work for your team.

Another resource was simply a critical set of eyes; I learned to ask for feedback sooner for in-progress work. Surface-level comments made me rethink deeper underlying issues.

Strong Opinions Loosely Held



Throughout the process I pushed hard for user needs, defending positions during team meetings and frequently adjusting between iterations. Even though my research wasn’t bulletproof, each small design decision was justifiable through it.

It was important to realize that snapshots of users could change drastically. That required rapidly adjusting to the new circumstances — but fighting just as hard for that new user.
