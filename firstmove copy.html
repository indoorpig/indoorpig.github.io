First Move

UX Design
July 2019
3 Weeks

Enabling Mobility




Mercedes-Benz as an automobile manufacturer faces an existential challenge through customers’ changing views of mobility.

I designed a new service offering in the form of a digital assistant with the Innovation Team at Mercedes-Benz.

Background



We started with a simple idea: what if Mercedes-Benz could help you get out your door in the morning faster - before even getting to your car?

In the face of ubiquitous rideshare and mobility options, we had to move past cars; we had to re-learn how users got from place to place.

Challenge




In three weeks, explore how we might facilitate mobility for users in the morning.


Our team at MBUSA looked to attract new customers - and provide value for existing ones by expanding our service offerings, and not just our cars. We wanted to explore a solution that MB has never been able to before.

My Role



I led design in my team of three - including a software engineer intern and a business intern.

I ran UX research for one week, synthesized data for another week, and built the design prototypes in the final week.

The Problem




Compromise isn't Enough



Mobility was a transition between places, and our hypothesis was simple.

People need help leaving home in the morning.

We confirmed through research users had too much to worry about in a short period. Naturally, users compromised by only completing some tasks, rarely getting everything done.

Trees, Not the Forest



We found users were focused on individual tasks with a short-term approach.

Their incremental, task-oriented mindset was out of necessity - they didn't have time for sense-making.

Summing Up



These understandings set our two primary constraints: low cognitive load in the moment and minimal interaction.

How might we help users accomplish more in the morning, getting them out the door happier and faster without adding tasks?

The Solution




First Move is a digital assistant that manages daily tasks by fitting into users’ daily routines.

Active Digital Assistance



First Move stands out from other digital assistants by automatically taking care of scheduled tasks — what we call Moves.

Rapid Move Creation



Create an automated task, or Move, quickly by selecting a service, toggling either AM or PM time slots, and filling out information.

Natural Reminders



Moves alert users — but only when relevant. Picking up milk on your way home in the afternoon? Receive the alert as you turn on the ignition of your Mercedes-Benz.

Automate Your Morning



Moves are more than just reminders. If a user wants to get coffee before work, they simply set the time they need to leave and create a Move to order coffee.

First Move stores that request and Leave Time, sending the coffee order at the specified time.

The user just has to leave on time to pick their coffee.

Multiple Tasks, On Time



What if a user needs to turn off the AC, order lunch, and call a rideshare before they leave?

Each Move triggers individually but based off a single Leave Time.

  • The rideshare Move accounts for estimated wait time and gets sent first.
  • The lunch Move is sent as the user leaves.
  • The smart home Moves are flipped a minute after the user leaves.

Recurring Moves



Got a morning routine? Users can set up repeating Moves with specific rules. If it ever gets cold on weekdays, users can always look forward to a preheated Mercedes — without ever making a Move.

Dashboard for Services



First Move acts as a home for third party services through quick downloads. Want to automate your Chick-Fil-A lunch order? Control IoT devices? Do it all in First Move.

How Did I Get Here




Transitions as Friction



We started by deconstructing mobility, the end-to-end process of getting from A to B.

One specific moment stood out: leaving a static state and starting a commute. The transition to the transition; the 3 minute before heading out the door.

Accounting for Variation



We understood mornings experiences varied depending on user schedules. To account for this, we classified and recruited 30 user interviews according to three categories:

  1. Daily 9-5 Commuters
  2. Variable or Work-from-Home Commuters
  3. Students

Defining Friction



One dad helped two children with different schedules get to school so he could leave for work. One woman packed different equipment every day for after-work activities.

Challenges like these centered around too many tasks in a short period. As a result, friction could be defined in 3 general elements:

Finding Our Users



Our interviews revealed four main user groups, defined by two factors.

  1. Available time vs. tasks needed to be done
  2. Stake behind the tasks

Realistically, we knew the limits of a 3 week MVP. Scoping was necessary, identifying one user group we could reliably target and solve for. These ended up being our "Stable Users."

Traditional personas bias toward demographic factors. To combat this, we employed modified JBTD framework.



With that, we had a complete picture of our user (stable, independent professionals) and their need (accomplishing more tasks in a morning).

Our asssumption was that by completing these tasks, transitions would be eased, enabling mobility.

Competitive Audit



What's out there? Tesla offers calendar sync, collecting user calendars and removing the need for user input. But assistance is limited to reminders and GPS.

Meanwhile, Siri, Google, and Alexa perform great - given users initiate. But with their short-sighted task flurry in mornings, users weren't initiating in the first place.

Flipping the Script


Existing assistants help on demand but users weren't initiating in the chaos of the morning. What if assistants could anticipate and provide actionable solutions?

Design




The Approach



Get testable wireframes ASAP and on the eyeballs of users, on whiteboards or lo-fi. With only one week, I had to gather actionable feedback and iterate — fast.

Whiteboard



The idea was that users would share tasks they needed completing. The product processes that task at the right time and provides a solution.

Visually expressed, tasks took the form of cards. Until needed, cards were archived. When the time came for a task to be completed, its card resurfaced — with yes or no actions.

Breaking this down, that meant there were two main challenges to be solved:

  1. Creating Moves (tasks) to be done.
  2. Activating created Moves at a specific time.

Un-Chore-ify Tasks



First up: easing task creation.

Ideally, events are imported from native iOS/Google calendars and translated into possible action items. One setting, one permission, no manual input for all future events.

Though not ideal, in-app touchpoints needed work. Creating one-off tasks, no matter how streamlined, conflicted with research insights. As a result, recurring events were heavily pushed.



Once created, Moves took the form of cards. Together, cards comprised a feed. Lists were stressful, but a stack of cards could be acted on or swiped past; ignored or circled back to.

To limit cognitive load, Moves were hidden until action was required.

Simplifying Triggers



Next up: triggers, or how and when tasks activate.

Data supplied to create Moves was now used for requests. As a demo, my teammate remotely scheduled, started, and pre-heated a C300 equipped with Mercedes Me.

The same underpinnings allowed for reminders, traffic alerts and ETAs, and third-party services (e.g. hailing an Uber). And with provided time, services could be effectively account for wait times.

Usability Testing



With an evaluation-ready wireframe, I tested prototypes with eight users.

Concerns of navigation and basic IA were apparent — but these were actually easy changes. Two interesting problems came with Move creation and interactions within the card feed.

Creating a single Move was like filling out a form.





There were too many options. Setting time was the main culprit; leaving users to decide the timing of events in context was too much of a strain.

Just as important, there was a fundamental disconnect between this form-like input and the resulting card output. The visual representation itself made it difficult for users to make connections in what they were doing.

Meanwhile, the feed had major interaction issues.





Discoverability was a major issue. While the original intent was to remove as much extraneous information as possible, too little information meant users couldn't find status updates — even on demand.

Once More, with Feeling



Finishing up my one-week prototype meant increasing visual fidelity and unity. The visual design was minimalist but cheerful — inspired heavily by Inbox.

With this framework, I could also address the aforementioned usability issues.
