First Move

UX Design
July 2019
3 Weeks

Enabling Mobility




Mercedes-Benz has traditionally been an automobile manufacturer. As the mobility landscape changes, so do they.

I designed a new breed of digital assistant with the Innovation Team at Mercedes-Benz.

Background



MB makes great cars. But transportation has already evolved.

The ubiquity of ridesharing and generational definitions of mobility are challenges that MB doesn’t meet as a traditional OEM.

We had to move past cars; we had to re-learn how users got from place to place.

Challenge




Facilitate mobility for users.


This project was initiated by the Innovation Team at MBUSA. Our goal was to create a new type of mobility product, leveraging our unfair advantage to attract new and existing customers.

My Role



I led UX design in my team of 3, alongside a software engineer intern and a business intern.

I ran UX research for one week, synthesized insights for another week, and built the design prototypes in the final week.

This project successfully gained approval by a GM in July 2019, received funding, and proceeded into its next stages.

The Problem




Transitions as Friction



We had a simple hypothesis: people need help when leaving from one place to another. Users simply had too many things to worry about in a short period of transition.

Our deep dive was into one specific moment: not the commute itself, but right before it. The preparation of leaving; the transition to the transition.

Defining Friction



One dad helped two children with different schedules get to school so he could leave for work. One woman packed different equipment every day for after-work activities.

Challenges like these centered around too many tasks in a short period. As a result, friction could be defined in 3 general elements:

Easing Task Flow



Users already had a high cognitive load. Introducing a solution could exacerbate existing problems. Our challenge was to simplify mornings while working with existing behavioral patterns of users.

Put simply: how can we get users out the door faster and happier — without giving them more things to do?

The Solution




First Move is a digital assistant that manages daily tasks by fitting into users’ daily routines.

Active Digital Assistance



First Move stands out from other digital assistants by automatically taking care of scheduled tasks — what we call Moves.

Rapid Move Creation



Create an automated task, or Move, quickly by selecting a service, toggling either AM or PM time slots, and filling out information.

Natural Reminders



Moves alert users — but only when relevant. Picking up milk on your way home in the afternoon? Receive the alert as you turn on the ignition of your Mercedes-Benz.

Automate Your Morning



Moves are more than just reminders. If a user wants to get coffee before work, they simply set the time they need to leave and create a Move to order coffee.

First Move stores that request and Leave Time, sending the coffee order at the specified time.

The user just has to leave on time to pick their coffee.

Multiple Tasks, On Time



What if a user needs to turn off the AC, order lunch, and call a rideshare before they leave?

Each Move triggers individually but based off a single Leave Time.

  • The rideshare Move accounts for estimated wait time and gets sent first.
  • The lunch Move is sent as the user leaves.
  • The smart home Moves are flipped a minute after the user leaves.

Recurring Moves



Got a morning routine? Users can set up repeating Moves with specific rules. If it ever gets cold on weekdays, users can always look forward to a preheated Mercedes — without ever making a Move.

Dashboard for Services



First Move acts as a home for third party services through quick downloads. Want to automate your Chick-Fil-A lunch order? Control IoT devices? Do it all in First Move.

How Did I Get Here




Deconstructing A to B



Understanding user needs meant deconstructing the definition of mobility into the transition between places.

Mornings were commonly shared experiences across commuters and acted as the bounds of our investigation. But these experiences varied depending on user schedules.

To account for this, we classified and recruited 30 user interviews into three categories:

  1. Daily 9-5 Commuters
  2. Variable or Work-from-Home Commuters
  3. Students

Commutes Aren't The Problem



Through user interviews, we learned commutes weren’t particularly stressful; they were zen spaces for many.

Instead, transitions before transitions were more stressful. These were the moments between static states and a commute: the 3 minute scramble of grabbing keys, shutting off the lights, and heading out the door.

Defining Users



Our team sorted qualitative data to create user groups through affinity mapping. Four main groups were constructed according to two factors:

  1. Available time vs. tasks needed to be done
  2. Stake behind the tasks

We targeted users in this middle persona grouping; users who were “just getting by.”

I knew relying heavily on traditional personas biases toward demographic factors. To combat this, we took a modified JBTD framework, mapping situations to motivations and outcomes.

The Need



On good days, users had just enough time in the morning. But more often than not, they struggled to fulfill all the simple tasks in the morning.

Our assumption was that users would reach for help in completing these tasks. Satisfying these would ease transitions, allowing them to kickstart their day: enabling mobility.

Design




Competitive Audit



Why don’t other solutions work? Current digital assistants perform suitably given user-initiated action — but they weren’t initiating during their mornings.

And while other products attempt to carve out new touchpoints (smart speakers in homes, virtual assistants on phones), our advantage were our cars — established touchpoints in their day.

Our Solution



Our solution stemmed from a simple, idealized vision.

We proposed an unobtrusive assistant to anticipate and execute tasks centered around context-sensitive triggers.

There were several prinicples based from research:

  1. High extraneous cognitive load meant the solution had to have simple interactions.
  2. The system must reach out first to capture user attention.
  3. System prompts must occur within a window of context-sensitivity to be perceived as solving ad hoc needs.
  4. System prompts outside this window were perceived as additional chores.

Break it Down



We chose a distinct visual and interaction style that diverged from existing MB products as a test for new customers the brand wasn't capturing.

There were two main parts to this system:

  1. Users create Moves (tasks) to be done.
  2. Moves are then activated at a specific time (Trigger)

Un-Chore-ify Tasks



First, I focused on how users created and managed tasks to be done.

Users’ threshold for what they were willing to do in the morning was extremely low. Users lose interest if the system:

  • prompted too often
  • asked users to do too much at once
  • took too much time to complete
Each morning, users had a list of to-do tasks. Organizing a feed of Moves meant deciding how they were displayed and changed the nature of the interaction. Users saw lists as intimidating first thing in the morning. Instead, the metaphor of cards were seen as “more friendly.”

A feed of cards were swiped left and right. Each card is completely ignore-able to further lower the cognitive load. Each card featured limited (max of 2), self-contained actions for a task.

Cards only showed up when it required user input. Otherwise, Moves were hidden in the main feed but scheduled. New Moves are created by swiping to the left.

Simplifying Triggers



Next, I needed to simplify triggers — how and when tasks would activate.

Timing was essential. Prompts during the right window was perceived as helpful - anything outside of that was annoyance. But having users determine that window meant higher cognitive loads.

Instead, I created two windows for tasks: morning (AM) or afternoon (PM) tasks, limiting user decisionm-making.

Users can’t create Moves more than a half-day in advance: this was intentional to create faster, on-the-fly interactions.

What we called "Leave Times" solved exact timing issues. Every AM or PM, a card asks when users plan to leave. This determines the exact time of Moves activate.

By breaking apart time calculation into two distinct streamlined interactions, we lowered the mental calculus for users.

Details and Nudges



To finish my quick, 1-week prototype, I focused on small details and nudges.

For example, completing all Moves and clearing the feed rewarded a micro-interaction of completing the day’s tasks. This touch was received well with users who felt delight in accomplishing something while on the move.

I also included an alternative PM color scheme helped visually distinguish the trigger selection. This also helped create a distinct micro-interaction during the Move creation process.
